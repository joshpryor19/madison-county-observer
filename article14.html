Richmond City Commission Meeting Erupts Into Full-Blown Wrestling Match

One man was fired without a reason, one was un-fired without an apology, and the meeting went so far off-script even the referee stopped enforcing rules.

By Staff Correspondent · RICHMOND, MADISON COUNTY

FILE 014 / STATUS RULED NO-CONTEST

RICHMOND — For the first six minutes, Tuesday's City Commission meeting looked exactly like every other one: roll call, approval of last month's minutes, a motion seconded by someone who wasn't fully listening. It held together right up until personnel matters hit the agenda. That was the last calm item on the docket.

First came a closed-door session that left a groundskeeper outside rubbing his neck.

When the doors opened, City Clerk Lisa Cassity took the mic and, ring-announcer boom and all, announced that a longtime Parks Department worker had been let go, “for reasons the board is not obligated to share.” No explanation followed. None was expected — the man was, in the language the building now uses without irony, quietly released from the card.

Then, not ten minutes later, came the reinstatement of Kevin Causey, Planning & Zoning's prodigal director, demoted weeks earlier for conduct nobody on the dais would name and now welcomed back to a full pop.

His manager, Commissioner Tammy Cole, worked the podium instead of him, holding a laminated population chart over her head like a title belt. “This city is growing,” she said. “A growing city needs a champion in that chair.” No consequence was attached. None was proposed.

It was watching those two decisions land back to back — one man shown the door with no reason given, another walked back in with none required — that something in Commissioner Jim Newby visibly snapped, and the last of the pretense broke completely. He stood up, tore open his dress shirt to reveal the Union Jack singlet of his former regional wrestling identity underneath, and informed the room exactly which version of him was now handling this. “You want to talk about who deserves what in this building?” he shouted.

“Let's talk about it as The British Bulldog, JACK!” When Mayor Robert “The Reverend” Blythe tried to move the meeting along by asking, as he now does at the top and close of every session, whether the room's hearts and minds were clear, Newby dropped to one knee, threw both arms wide, and roared back, “CANNNNN YOU TELLLL THAT HEARTS AND MINDS ARE CLEEEEAAAARRRR?!”

A junior staffer, new enough not to know this is how it goes now, dropped a full stack of agendas.

From there the whole meeting went off the rails. Two zoning commissioners, working as a tag team the rest of the building calls The Recusal Brothers, stormed the podium mid-meeting demanding every board member cut a receipts promo justifying every vote, forever, aiming their fire at two colleagues they wouldn't name but clearly meant.

Blythe let it go almost four full minutes before losing all composure. “What you're telling me, brother, is you want a man put on trial for voting different than you,” he said, and brought his open palm down on the table — the move staff quietly call the Hand Slammer — hard enough to send the water pitcher airborne, crack the table's left leg, and send the meeting into what the clerk's minutes describe only as “a period of no rules.” “If you don't like this board, brother, hand in your gear!” he roared, then wheeled on his city manager.

“Hold me back, Rob. Hold me back!”

Rob “The Rock” Minerich obliged by clearing the length of the bench with a running leg drop, missed his landing, caught a folding chair on the way down, and used it anyway — a legal move, apparently, under whatever rules were left standing by that point.

The Recusal Brothers didn't stick around to eat it. One shouted “You've heard the last of me” on his way to the exit; the other, mid-retreat, stopped to shake Minerich's hand, got pulled into an unscheduled piledriver for his trouble, popped back up unbothered, and kept walking like it hadn't happened.

By then Causey, still taped at the mouth from his own demotion hearing, had apparently seen enough. He tore the tape off, grabbed the mic nobody handed him, and cut what witnesses are calling the best promo of the year — twelve unbroken minutes naming names, dates, and enough F bombs to make a sailor cry — before City Attorney Chris Johnson, functioning as the evening's de-facto referee, threw up both hands and called the entire session a no-contest, on the grounds that there was no longer a ring, a rulebook, or a quorum anybody could verify.

“CANNNNN YOU TELLLL THAT HEARTS AND MINDS ARE CLEEEEAAAARRRR?!”
— JIM NEWBY, THE BRITISH BULLDOG

Cassity gaveled it closed at 11:52 p.m. Newby never got the memo. Long after the room had emptied, staff say, he was still on the arena side of the dais in the Union Jack singlet, dropping to one knee and asking a row of stacked chairs whether their hearts and minds were clear. A rematch has already been penciled in for next month's regular session, main event and all.
